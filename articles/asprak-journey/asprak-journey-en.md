## ğŸ§  Semester 4 â€” Data Structures and the Challenge of Simplifying the Complex

<img width="960" height="1280" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/dd569434-0607-4bd4-99d3-1ed47727d36a" />

Entering Semester 4, I returned to teaching. This time, the material leveled up:  
**Data Structures & Algorithms**.

The challenge here was no longer *what* to teach, but **how to explain it**.

Topics like:
- Stack
- Queue
- Linked List  

are easy to memorize, but **hard to understand** without real-world context.

I started asking myself:
> "How can I make this material relevant to the industry?"

So I began:
- relating stacks to call stacks in the backend,
- explaining queues through real-world queuing systems,
- teaching through **live coding**, analogies, and even small games.

My hope was simple:  
if they ever forget the theory later, **the logic would still remain**.

In Semester 5, I taught **Database Systems**.

My reason was clear and very personal:  
while working as a Software Engineer, I realized **SQL is gold** â€” but it's often underestimated on campus.

There are many things rarely touched upon seriously:
- `CASCADE`
- `VIEW`
- `TRANSACTION`
- `GROUP BY` & `HAVING`

Yet in the industry, these are exactly what frequently appear.

I didn't want them to just know:
> "SELECT * FROM table"

I wanted them to **understand why a query can be dangerous, expensive, or powerful**.

In this class, I did a lot of:
- one-on-one mentoring,
- dissecting query errors together,
- and explaining databases as the foundation of data science & system design.

## ğŸš€ Semester 7 â€” GitHub Classroom and the Legacy I Want to Leave

![WhatsApp Image 2025-12-19 at 3 38 53 PM](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/5b4936bc-e73c-4208-b54d-6493faaa8544)

In Semester 7, I taught **two classes simultaneously**:
- Web-Based Programming
- Object-Oriented Programming

This was when I made an important decision:  
**to use GitHub Classroom**.

It wasn't just a tool.  
It was a **legacy**.

The challenge was immense:
- students in Semesters 3 & 5 **had never been taught Git/GitHub before**,
- the material was vital but often skipped,
- it felt overwhelming at first.

But gradually:
- they became accustomed to repositories,
- started understanding version control,
- learned collaboration,
- and got to know industry standards early on.

I realize they might forget my name someday.  
But if one day they say:

> "I'm glad I was taught GitHub back then..."

That would be enough.

I hope this becomes:
- a long-term asset,
- knowledge that keeps flowing,
- and hopefully... a **charity that never ceases (amal jariyah)**.
